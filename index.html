Banks have barriers, but your habits matter most.

Here are 7 Security Tips

Use Strong Passwords

Protecting your online accounts is crucial. Think of your passwords as keys to your digital life – keep them secure!

  • Don't share your passwords or leave them exposed. Use a unique, strong password for every account, and change them regularly.
  • Consider a password manager to create and store individual, complex passwords for you. Many popular devices and providers offer them.
  • Make your passwords tough to crack:
    • At least 10 characters long.
    • Mix letters, numbers, and special characters.
    • Be unpredictable – avoid names, birthdays, or common words.
  • Never reuse passwords. If one account is compromised, all your others could be too.
  • Legitimate companies won't ask for your password via phone, text, or email. If they do, it's a scam.
  • Always keep your passwords in a secure, hidden place.

Enable Multifactor Authentication

Think of multi-factor authentication (MFA) as an extra lock on your digital doors. Instead of just a password, MFA uses at least one more way to verify it's really you. This could be a code sent to your phone, a fingerprint scan, or even facial recognition. Adding these extra layers makes it much harder for cybercriminals to break into your accounts, even if they manage to get your password.

Only Share Personal Info on Secure Websites

When you're online shopping or banking, only enter personal information on websites that use encryption.

How to tell if a site is secure: Look for "https://" at the start of the web address. The "s" means it's secure.

Always check for "https://" on every page where you enter or view personal data, not just the login page. If any part of your session isn't encrypted, your account could be vulnerable.

Maintain Device Security

For online banking or shopping, **security software is a must**. This includes antivirus, anti-spam, and spyware detection. Keep this software **updated automatically**.

Install **security updates** on all your devices as soon as you get notifications. Check your computer and software provider's websites for more security tips.

If you're using **cloud storage for personal financial information**, do your homework. Ensure the provider uses **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** and **data encryption**. Read their terms of service carefully, including all costs, before agreeing.

Click smart.

The best way to protect yourself from bad links is to **not click them**. Even if a link looks real, it's safest to avoid it.

Instead of clicking a suspicious link, replying to a strange email, or opening an attachment from someone you don't know, **go directly to the company's official website or app** to check the information. Clicking on fake messages can put malware on your device or expose your personal info.
